Introduction and Motivation

Topological quantum phase transition:

  • Zero temperature.
  • Vanishing of the energy gap in critical regions, and ground state degeneracy.
  • Non-local order parameter (topological invariants).

Results:

  1. We show the existence of Bogoliubov automorphisms relating states belonging to the same phase of matter, and their relationship with a $\Z_2$ index.
  2. We also show that a change in the index implies the spectral gap closing, and we give a general expression for the state at this critical condition.
  3. We find a relationship between the Ulhmann phase and the longitudinal magnetization for the Ising XY model.
  4. Making use of the Gibbs state purification process we show a convenient way to obtain the above result for the geometric phase.

Self-dual $\CAR$ algebra

Let $\a\colon\K\to\K$ be a conjugation or antiunitary involution on $\K$, i.e., an antilinear operator such that $\a^{2}=\I$ and

$$\begin{equation*} \inner{\a\varphi_{1},\a\varphi_{2}}_{\K}=\inner{\varphi_{2},\varphi_{1}}_{\K},\qquad \varphi_{1},\varphi_{2}\in\K. \end{equation*}$$
Self-dual $\CAR$ algebra

A self-dual $\CAR$ algebra $\asd\equiv (\asd,+,\cdot ,*)$ is a $\C$-algebra generated by a unit $\I$ and a family $\{B(\varphi )\}_{\varphi \in \K}$ of elements satisfying conditions:

  1. The map $\varphi \mapsto B\left(\varphi\right) ^{*} $ is (complex) linear.
  2. $B(\varphi)^{*}=B(\a(\varphi))$ for any $\varphi \in \K$.
  3. The family $\{B(\varphi)\}_{\varphi\in\K}$ satisfies the $\CAR$: For any $\varphi_{1},\varphi _{2}\in\K$, $$\begin{equation*} B(\varphi_{1})B(\varphi_{2})^{*}+B(\varphi_{2})^{*}B(\varphi_{1})=\left\langle \varphi_{1},\varphi_{2}\right\rangle_{\K}\,\I. \end{equation*}$$
Araki 1968

Basis projections

Basis projections
A basis projection associated with $(\K,\a)$ is an orthogonal projection $P\in\BL(\K)$ satisfying $\a P\a=P^{\bot}\equiv\I-P$. We denote by $\H$ the range $\mathrm{ran}(P)$ of the basis projection $P$. The set of all basis projections associated with $(\K,\a)$ will be denoted by $\fp$.

We can identify $\K$ with,

$$\begin{equation*}\K\equiv\H\oplus\overline{\H}\end{equation*}.$$

Then, we have the equivalence,

$$\begin{equation*} B\left(\varphi\right)\equiv B_{P}(\varphi):= a\left(P\varphi\right) +a\left(\a P^{\bot}\varphi\right)^{*}. \end{equation*}$$
Araki 1968

Bogoliubov transformation

Bogoliubov transformation

For any unitary operator $U\in\BL(\K)$ such that $U\a=\a U$, the family of elements $\{B(U\varphi)\}_{\varphi\in\K}$ together with the unit $\I$, generates $\asd$. Such a unitary operator $U\in \BL(\K)$ is named a Bogoliubov transformation, and the unique $^{*}$-automorphism $X_{U}$ such that

$$\begin{equation*}X_{U}\left( B(\varphi )\right) =B(U\varphi ),\qquad \varphi \in \K,\end{equation*}$$ is called in this case a Bogoliubov $^{*}$-automorphism.

Note that

$$\begin{equation*}\det\left(U\right)=\det\left(\a U\a\right)=\overline{\det\left(U\right)}=\pm 1\end{equation*}$$

For any pair $P_{1},P_{2}\in\fp$ there is a Bogoliubov transformation $U$ such that $P_{2}=U^{*}P_{1}U$.

Araki 1968

Bilinear Hamiltonians

Bilinear elements of self-dual CAR algebra
Given an orthonormal basis $\{\psi_{i}\}_{i\in I}$ of $\K$, we define the bilinear element associated with $h\in \BL(\K)$ to be $$\begin{align*} b(h) := \sum\limits_{i,j\in I}\left\langle \psi _{i},h\psi _{j}\right\rangle_{\K}\B\left(\psi_{j}\right)\B\left(\psi_{i}\right)^{*}. \end{align*}$$

Bilinear Hamiltonians are then defined as bilinear elements associated with self-dual Hamiltonians s.t. $h=h^{*}\in \BL(\K)$ and $\a h\a=-h$.

$$\begin{equation*}\tau _{t}(A):= \e^{-\ii tb(h)}A\e^{\ii tb(h)}\ ,\qquad A\in \asd(\K,\a),\ t\in\R.\end{equation*}$$
Araki 1968

Symbols and quasi-free states

A quasi-free state $\omega$ is uniquely defined by its so-called symbol, that is, a positive operator $S\in\BL(\K)$ such that

$$\begin{equation*}0\leq S\leq \I\qquad\text{and}\qquad S+\a S\a=\I,\end{equation*}$$

through the conditions

$$\begin{equation*} \omega\left(B(\varphi _{1})B(\a\varphi_{2})\right)=\left\langle \varphi _{1},S\varphi_{2}\right\rangle_{\K},\quad\varphi_{1},\varphi_{2}\in\K. \end{equation*}$$

For any $\beta \in (0,\infty)$ and any self-dual Hamiltonian $h$ on $(\K,\a)$ the positive operator $S_\beta:=(\I+\e^{-\beta h})^{-1}$ satisfies the conditions above and is the symbol of a quasi-free state $\omega_\beta$ satisfying

$$\begin{equation*} \omega_\beta(A)=\frac{\text{tr}\left(A\exp\left(\frac{\beta }{2}b(h)\right) \right)}{\text{tr}\left( \exp\left(\frac{\beta}{2}b(h)\right)\right)},\qquad A\in \asd. \end{equation*}$$
Araki 1970

Canonical purification

Consider $P_S\in\mathcal{B}(\K\oplus\K)$ given by:

$$\begin{equation*} P_S := \left( \begin{array}{cc} S & \sqrt{S} \sqrt{\I - S} \\ \sqrt{S} \sqrt{\I - S} & \I - S \end{array}\right), \end{equation*}$$

$P_S$ is a basis projection on $(\widetilde{\K},\widetilde{\Gamma})$, where $\widetilde{\K}:=\K\oplus\K$ and $\widetilde{\Gamma}:=\Gamma\oplus(-\Gamma)$:

$$\begin{equation*} P_S^2=P_S=P_S^*,\quad \widetilde{\Gamma}P_S\widetilde{\Gamma}=\I-P_S, \end{equation*}$$
THEOREM Purification (Araki 1970)

The restriction of the Fock state $\omega_{P_S}$ of $\A_{_{SD}}(\widetilde{\K},\widetilde{\Gamma})$ to $\A_{_{SD}}({\K},{\Gamma})$ is the quasi free state $\omega_S$.

Araki 1970

Quasi-free ground states

$$h=\int_{\sp(h)}\lambda dE_{\lambda}=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\lambda dE_{\lambda},$$

where $\sp(h)$ denotes the spectrum of $h$. Thus, one verify that

$$\begin{equation*} \a E_{\lambda}\a=E_{-\lambda}\quad\text{for all}\quad \lambda\in\R\quad\text{and}\quad E_{0}+E_{-}+E_{+}=\I. \end{equation*}$$
Quasi-free ground states
The state $\omega\in\asd^{*}$ satisfying $\ii\omega(A^{*}\delta(A))\geq0$, and $$\begin{equation*} \omega\left(B(\varphi_{1})B(\a\varphi_{2})\right)=\left\langle \varphi_{1},E_{+}\varphi_{2}\right\rangle_{\K},\quad\varphi_{1},\varphi_{2}\in\K. \end{equation*}$$ it will be called a quasi-free ground state. The set of all quasi-free ground states it will denoted by $\mathfrak{q}\states\subset\states$.
Araki 1970

$\Z_2$-Index

Assumptions

Take $\cal:=[0,1]$.

  • $\mathbf h:=\{h_{s}\}_{s\in\cal}\in\BL(\K)$ is a differentiable family of self-dual Hamiltonians such that $\partial \mathbf h:=\{\partial_{s}h_{s}\}_{s\in\cal}\in\BL(\K)$.
  • For the infinite volume, we assume that the sequences of self-dual Hamiltonians $h_{s,\,L}\colon\cal\to\BL(\K)$ and $\partial_{s}h_{s,\,L}\colon\cal\to\BL(\K)$ are convergent in norm and pointwise, that is, $\lim\limits_{L\to\infty}h_{s,L}=h_{s}$ and $\lim\limits_{L\to\infty}\partial_{s}h_{s,L}=\partial_{s}h_{s}$ in the norm sense.
  • Gap asumption: $g_s\in\R^{+}$ s.t. $g_s:=\inf\left\{\epsilon>0\ \colon\left[-\epsilon,\epsilon\right]\cap\sp(h_s)\neq\emptyset\right\}$.

Now, for any self-dual Hilbert space $(\K,\a)$, take $P_{1}, P_{2}\in\fp$ basis projections, the "$\Z_{2}$-projection index" $\sigma\colon\fp\times\fp\to\Z_{2}$ is the map defined by:

$$\begin{equation*}\sigma(P_{1},P_{2}):=(-1)^{\dim(P_{1}\land P_{2}^{\perp})}=\det(U).\end{equation*}$$
Araki-Matsui 1985

Phases of matter

Phase of the Matter

Let $I\equiv[0,1]$ and $\{h_{s}\}_{s\in \cal}\in\B(\K)$ be a continuous family of self-dual Hamiltonians on $(\K,\Gamma)$. We will say that $h_{s}$ is an $s$-gapped Hamiltonian if the gap assumption is satisfied for any $s\in \cal$. Define now an equivalence relation on the set of gapped self-dual Hamiltonians, as follows. If $h^{(0)},h^{(1)}\in \BL(\K)$ are two gapped self-dual Hamiltonians, then $h^{(0)}\sim h^{(1)}$ if and only if the following conditions are satisfied:

  1. There is a continuous family of self-dual Hamiltonians on $(\K,\a)$, where $h_s$ is gapped for all $s\in \cal$;
  2. There is a lower bound $g\in\R^{+}$, independent of $s$, in the sense that $\inf\limits_{s\in\cal}g_{s}\geq g>0$;
  3. $h_0=h^{(0)}$ and $h_1=h^{(1)}$.
THEOREM 1 (Existence of automorphism)
For any $s\in \cal$, the associated representations $\pi_0$ of $h_{0}$ and $\pi_s$ of $h_{s}$ are unitarily equivalent, the unitary operator $V_{s}\in\BL(\K)$ implementing the equivalence is Bogoliubov, and satisfies the differential equation: $$\begin{equation*} \partial_sV_s=-\ii D_{g,s} V_s,\quad V_0=\I, \end{equation*}$$ where $D_{g,s}\in\BL(\K)$ is given by $$\begin{equation*}D_{g,s}:=\int_\R\tau_t(\partial_s h_s)W_g(t)dt=\int_\R\e^{ith_s}(\partial_s h_s)\e^{-it h_s}W_g(t)dt\end{equation*}$$ and the weight function $W_g\in \L^1(\R)$ whose Fourier transform $\hat{W}_g\in \L^1(\R)$ is such that: $$\begin{equation*} \hat{W}_g(\mu)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_\R \e^{\ii t\mu}W_g(t)dt=-\frac{\ii}{\sqrt{2\pi}\mu}. \end{equation*}$$

We can show that $E_{+,s}=V_sE_{+,0}V_s^*$, and $\det(V_s)=1$ for all $s\in\cal$.

THEOREM 2 (Gap closing)

Suppose that $h_{0}$ and $h_{1}$ are gapped, and their corresponding basis projections satisfy $\sigma (E_{+,0}, E_{+,1})=-1$. Then there exists $\widetilde s\in \mathring{\cal}$ such that $0\in\sp(h_{\widetilde s})$, i.e., such that $E_{0,\widetilde s}\neq \emptyset$. Furthermore, the path $\mathbf{h}$ induces, in a natural way, a family of ground states for $h_{\widetilde s}$ which are generically mixed states. More specifically, we have:

  1. The gap closing at $\tilde s$ induces a splitting of the one-particle Hilbert space as the direct sum of two $\Gamma$-invariant subspaces, $\K=\K_0\oplus \K_1$, as well as a factorization $\A\cong\A_{0}\otimes \A_{1}$, where $\A_{0}:= \asd(\K_0,\a)$ and $\A_{1}:= \asd(\K_1,\a)$.
  2. The set of ground states associated with the self-dual Hamiltonian $H_{\tilde s,\infty}$ have the form $$ \widetilde\omega(a_1a_0)=\omega_{\widetilde E_+}(a_1)\phi_0(a_0), $$ where $a_1\in \A_{1}$, $\omega_{\widetilde E_+}\in\states$ is a quasi-free ground state on $\A_1$ associated with a basis projection $\widetilde E_+\in \mathfrak{p}(\mathcal K_1,\Gamma)$, and $a_0\in \A_{0}$ with $\phi_0\in\states$ an arbitrary state on $\A_0$. In particular, the right and left limits $\lim\limits_{s\to \tilde s^\pm} E_{+,s}$ single out states $\omega_0^{+}$ and $\omega_0^{-}$ on $\A_0$, with $\omega_0^{+}\neq\omega_0^{-}$. In this sense, the path $\mathbf{h}$ induces, in a natural way, the following family of ground states for $h_{\tilde s}$: $$\begin{equation*} \widetilde\omega_\lambda(a_1a_0)=\omega_{\widetilde E_+}(a_1)\left[\lambda \omega_0^{+}(a_0) +(1-\lambda)\omega_0^{-}(a_0)\right],\qquad \lambda\in[0,1]. \end{equation*}$$

Geometric phases

  • (a) $X_\psi=\dot{\psi},\ g(X_\psi,X_\phi):=\frac{1}{2}\left(\inner{\dot\phi,\dot\psi}+\inner{\dot\psi,\dot\phi}\right)$, then, $$A_B(X_\psi):=\inner{d\psi,\psi},\quad \phi=\arg(\inner{\psi_0,\psi_1})$$
  • (b) $X_w=\dot{w},\ g(X_{w_1},X_{w_2}):=\frac{1}{2}\text{tr}\left(\dot w_1^*\dot w_2+\dot w_2^*\dot w_1\right)$, then, $$A_U(X_w):=\left[dw,w\right],\quad \phi=\arg\left[\text{tr}(w_0^*w_1)\right]$$

1d Ising XY model with transverse field

$$\begin{equation*} H=-\frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=-L}^{L-1}\left(\frac{1+\gamma}{2}\sigma_{j}^x\sigma_{j+1}^x+\frac{1-\gamma}{2}\sigma_{j}^y\sigma_{j+1}^y+\lambda\sigma_j^z\right), \quad P.B.C. \end{equation*}$$

$H\in\BL(\H_T)$, with $\mathcal{H}_T=\bigotimes^N\mathbb{C}^2$. After Jordan-Wigner transformation,

$$\begin{equation*} H=\sum_{i,j=1}^N a_i^{\dagger}M_{ij} a_j+\frac{1}{2}(a_i^{\dagger}N_{ij} a_j^{\dagger}-a_iN_{ij} a_j). \end{equation*}$$

$\mathcal{F}_-(\H)=\bigoplus_{i=0}^N\bigwedge^{i}\H$, $\H=l^2(\Lambda)$, $\Lambda:=\{1,2,\dots,N\}\subset\mathbb{Z}$. Applying the self-dual formalism $H=b(h)$ with

$$\begin{equation*} h=\left(\begin{array}{cc} M & N \\ -N & -M \end{array}\right). \end{equation*}$$

$h\in\mathcal{B}(\K)$, and $\K=\H\oplus\overline{\H}$.

Thermal states

After Fourier transformation, the spectral decomposition of self-dual one-particle hamiltonian is

$$\begin{equation*} \hat{h}_k = \left(\begin{array}{cc} \hat{M}_k & \hat{N}_k \\ -\hat{N}_k & -\hat{M}_k \end{array}\right) = \dfrac{\Lambda_k}{2} \left( E_{+,k} - E_{-,k} \right), \end{equation*}$$ $$\hat{M}_k=\cos{k}-\lambda,\ \hat{N}_k=\gamma\sin{k},\ \Lambda_k = \sqrt{(\cos{k}-\lambda)^2 + (\gamma \sin{k})^2},$$ $$\begin{equation*} E_{\pm,k} = \dfrac{1}{2} \left( \I_{_K} \pm \left[ \dfrac{\gamma \sin{k}}{\Lambda_k}\sigma_y + \dfrac{(\cos{k} - \lambda)}{\Lambda_k}\sigma_z \right] \right). \end{equation*}$$

Define $\lambda_{\pm,k} := (1+e^{\pm \beta \Lambda_k})^{-1}$, then the symbol $S_k= (\I+e^{-\beta \widehat{h}_k })^{-1}\in \BL(\K)$ is

$$\begin{equation*} S_k = \lambda_{+,k} E_{+,k} + \lambda_{-,k} E_{-,k}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\I+\tanh(\beta\Lambda_k)\hat{G}_k\cdot\vec{\sigma}\right),\ \hat{G}_k=\tfrac{1}{\Lambda_k}(0,\hat{M}_k,\hat{N}_k) \end{equation*}$$

Ulhmann-Dabrowski geometric phase

  • Notice that $E_\pm - E_\mp = \pm \hat{G}_k \cdot \vec{\sigma}$, then $J=\mathrm{i}(2E_+-\I)=\mathrm{i}\hat{G}_k\cdot\sigma$, $dJ=2\mathrm{i}dE_+.$ Define $F_k(\beta):=1-\operatorname{sech}(\beta\Lambda_k)$, and the connection is written by $$A_U=F_k(\beta)JdJ.$$
  • Define the function $f(\gamma,\lambda,\beta)$ through $$\oint A_U \equiv \mathrm{i} f(\gamma,\lambda,\beta) \sigma_x,\quad f(\gamma,\lambda,\beta):= \oint \frac{\gamma}{2\Lambda_k^2} \left( 1 - \dfrac{1}{\cosh(\beta\Lambda_k)} \right) \left(1-\lambda \cos k \right) dk.$$
  • The (unitary) holonomy $$V_1=\mathcal{T}\mathrm{e}^{\oint A_U} = \cos{(f)}\I + \mathrm{i} \sin{(f)} \sigma_x,$$
  • Taking $S_0 = S(k=0)$, then

    $$\begin{equation*} \phi_U = \arg \left[ \operatorname{tr} \left(S_0 \mathrm{e}^{\oint A_u}\right)\right] = \arg \left[ \text{tr} \left(S_0 \cos{(f)} \right)\right] = \arg \left[\cos{(f)}\right], \end{equation*}$$

Geometric phase from Kato's theory

Kato's transport equation is:

$$\begin{equation*} \dot{V}(s) = [\dot{P}(s), P(s)] V(s),\quad \dot{V} = \frac{dV}{ds},\quad V(0)=\I, \end{equation*}$$

The connection one-form is $A_K(X_P)= [d P, P]$. Taking a closed curve $\gamma: s \in \cal \mapsto \gamma(s):=P_s = V_s P_{0} V_s^\dagger$, where $V_s\equiv V(s)$ satisfies the transport equation. We obtain the holonomy $\mathbb{U}_h=V_{1}=\mathcal{T}e^{\oint \ii [d P, P] }$, where $\mathcal{T}$ is the time-ordering operator.

Relation Kato - Ulhman: $\sp{(\mathbb{U}_h )} = \sp{(U_h)}$, and

$$\begin{equation*} \text{tr}_{\widetilde{\K}}\left(\mathbb{E}P_0 \mathbb{U}_h\right) = \text{tr}_{\K} \left( S_0 U_h \right) \end{equation*}$$

where $\mathbb{U}_h $ is the holonomy on $P_S\in\BL(\widetilde{\mathcal{K}})$, $U_h$ is the Uhlmann's holonomy on $S\in\BL(\K)$, and $\mathbb{E}\widetilde{\mathcal{K}} = \mathcal{K} \oplus 0$.

Kato 1980, Corach-Porta-Recht 1990

$Z_2$-index and gemetric phase at zero temperature

Finite case:

$$\begin{equation*} U = \bigoplus_k U_k\end{equation*}$$ $$\begin{equation*} \det(U) = \displaystyle \prod_k \det(U_k) = sgn(1-\lambda), \end{equation*}$$

Geometric phase

$$\begin{equation*} \varphi = arg \left[ \text{tr}_{_\K} \left( E_{\pm,0} U_h \right) \right] = \left\lbrace \begin{array}{rcl} \pi, & & sgn(1-\lambda)=-1 \\ 0, & & sgn(1-\lambda)=1\end{array}\right. \end{equation*}$$

Conclusions and Perspectives

  1. The absolute $\Z_2$ index classifies the phases of matter in fermionic systems given by bilinear Hamiltonians.
  2. Ground states in a critical region can be written as a convex combination of states in the phases.
  3. We can calculate the geometric phase of (thermal) mixed states from the canonical purification in the self-dual formalism using Kato theory.
  4. The geometric phase extends the role of the index to finite temperature states and classifies the Ising XY model regimes.
  • Introduction of symmetries, connection with K-theory and non-commutative geometry, the classification of topological materials.
  • Generalization of the calculation of the geometric phase with the Kato theory and the self-dual formalism.
